Descriptions

Actinic Keratoses and Intraepithelial Carcinoma

These lesions are variants of squamous cell carcinoma that have surface scaling and can be pigmented or nonpigmented. Actinic keratoses are common on the face and intraepithelial carcinoma is common on other body sites.

Basal Cell Carcinoma

This appears as raised open sores, red patches or shiny bumps that may ooze or bleed. They are typically found in sun-exposed areas of the body. In patients with darker skin, about half of basal cell carcinoma lesions are pigmented brown in colour.

Benign Keratosis

This is a generic class with subgroups that look different, but are biologically similar. Seborrheic keratoses appears with age and varies with size and colour. Solar lentigo is a flat and dark patch, while lichen-planus like keratoses (LPLK) is small and varies from reddish brown to greyish/purple.

Dermatofibroma

Dermatofibroma develops reticular lines at the periphery and a central white patch. It appears as a small bump beneath the skin that varies in colour and is commonly found on the arms, lower legs and upper back of adults.

Melanocytic nevi

These are benign neoplasms of melanocytes and appear in a myriad of variants that may differ significantly from a dermatoscopic point of view. They are usually symmetric with regard to the distribution of color and structure.

Melanoma

Melanoma is a malignant neoplasm derived from melanocytes that may appear in different variants. They are typically asymmetrical with uneven or scalloped borders, consist of multiple shades of brown and can change in size, shape, color or elevation.

Vascular Skin Lesions

This category includes three subgroups. Angiomas are bright red or purple and may be slightly raised from the skin. Angiokeratomas are small, dome-like, dark spots that may appear in clusters. Pyogenic granulomas are shiny, small, red lumps that may occasionally bleed.
